# The Movies of the 1980s

## 33. [Caddyshack (1980)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0080487/)

**Plot in plain language.** A young caddy at a snooty country club tries to win a college scholarship while a vulgar new-money real-estate developer (Rodney Dangerfield) torments the old-money members, the assistant groundskeeper (Bill Murray) wages a one-man war against a gopher, and Chevy Chase plays a zen golf savant. It is barely a movie. It is more like four comedians improvising in the same parking lot.

**Why it stuck.** This is the foundational text of Gen X dad-humor. If you have ever heard a man in cargo shorts narrate his own golf swing, he learned to do that from Caddyshack. The Bill Murray gopher subplot, the Rodney Dangerfield one-liners, and the Ted Knight slow burn formed a comedy template that powered the next 25 years of sports comedies.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Be the ball." — Chase to Michael O'Keefe, sincerely teaching golf as meditation. Now used to mock anyone giving over-spiritual sports advice.
- "It's in the hole!" — Murray narrating an imaginary Masters victory while beheading flowers with a grass whip. Deployed any time anyone sinks a putt, a paper-ball free throw, or a bottle cap into a trash can.
- "Cinderella story, outta nowhere…" — same scene. The setup before "it's in the hole." Older guys use the whole monologue verbatim.
- "So I got that goin' for me, which is nice." — Murray's caddy delivers this after a story about a lama (the Dalai Lama, played for laughs) stiffing him on a tip. Used universally to acknowledge a small consolation prize.
- "Hey everybody, we're all gonna get laid!" — Rodney Dangerfield reading a club document out of context.

**Where it pops up now.** Every golf telecast eventually quotes it. Bill Murray's career-long persona — distracted, mumbling, slightly menacing — starts here. The "gopher" idea echoes in every modern animated nuisance-animal subplot.

## 34. [Airplane! (1980)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0080339/)

**Plot in plain language.** A traumatized ex-fighter pilot has to land a passenger jet after the crew gets food poisoning. That's the straight version. The actual movie is a non-stop parody of 1970s disaster films with a joke about every two seconds, including visual jokes happening in the background while the dialogue jokes are happening in the foreground.

**Why it stuck.** Airplane! invented a comedy style — the rapid-fire absurdist parody — that defined the next 30 years. Without it there is no Naked Gun, no Hot Shots, no Wet Hot American Summer, no Family Guy cutaways. Every Gen X kid memorized chunks of it because cable played it constantly.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Surely you can't be serious." / "I am serious. And don't call me Shirley." — The platonic ideal of a setup-and-payoff joke. Still the default response to anyone who starts a sentence with the word *surely*.
- "Looks like I picked the wrong week to quit sniffing glue." — The air-traffic controller (Lloyd Bridges) escalating his vices as the situation worsens. The construction "picked the wrong week to quit X" is now a generic format.
- "Don't call me Shirley" alone is a top-five Gen X reflex.

**Where it pops up now.** Anytime someone starts a sentence with "surely," half the room is silently mouthing the rest.

## 35. [The Empire Strikes Back (1980)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0080684/)

**Plot in plain language.** The middle Star Wars movie. The good guys lose, Luke gets his hand cut off, and Darth Vader reveals he is Luke's father. If you somehow do not know this, please forgive the spoiler — the statute of limitations expired around 1985.

**Why it stuck.** This is the moment Star Wars went from a fun popcorn movie to a Generation-defining mythology. Gen X kids grew up reenacting the Hoth battle in snowbanks and quoting Yoda backwards.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "I am your father." — Frequently misquoted as "Luke, I am your father." Used by every dad ever, in the deepest voice possible, while pointing at his own kid.
- "Do. Or do not. There is no try." — Yoda, instructing Luke. Now standard motivational-poster fodder.
- "Never tell me the odds." — Han Solo, dismissing C-3PO's statistics. Used by gamblers, entrepreneurs, and anyone about to do something dumb.

**Where it pops up now.** Every parody of fatherhood reveals. Every motivational coach quoting Yoda. Star Wars references are basically a second language at this point.

## 36. [Raiders of the Lost Ark (1981)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0082971/)

**Plot in plain language.** Archaeologist Indiana Jones races Nazis to find the Ark of the Covenant. Snakes are involved. Faces melt.

**Why it stuck.** Harrison Ford's slightly exhausted, slightly sarcastic action hero became the blueprint for every reluctant hero since. Also: Indiana Jones costumes have been a Halloween staple for 40 straight years.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Snakes. Why'd it have to be snakes?" — Indy looking into a pit. Used by every person discovering something they specifically did not want to discover.
- "I'm making this up as I go." — Used by anyone admitting they have no plan.

**Where it pops up now.** Every adventure movie owes it a royalty. The Indy whip-and-fedora silhouette is shorthand for "old-school action hero."

## 37. [Stripes (1981)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0083316/)

**Plot in plain language.** Two unemployable goofballs (Bill Murray and Harold Ramis) join the Army on a whim. Hijinks ensue.

**Why it stuck.** It cemented the Bill Murray smirk as a generational mood — bored, smarter than everyone, willing to do almost anything for a laugh.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "That's the fact, Jack!" — Recited by John Candy's Ox during basic training. Used as a punctuation mark when someone is correct.
- "Lighten up, Francis." — A drill sergeant to a thin-skinned recruit named Francis. Now the universal Gen X way of telling someone to chill out — *especially* to a man.
- "Chicks dig me, because I rarely wear underwear…" — John Candy's intro speech, frequently lifted as an absurd party line.

**Where it pops up now.** "Lighten up, Francis" is still in heavy rotation in every office, gym, and Slack channel.

## 38. [Fast Times at Ridgemont High (1982)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0083929/)

**Plot in plain language.** Cameron Crowe's loosely-connected slice-of-life about California high schoolers in 1981 — Spicoli the stoner surfer, the smart girl who has a complicated year, the older brother with a job at the burger place, and a parade of side characters. Based on Crowe going undercover at a real high school.

**Why it stuck.** This is the first movie that made high school feel real on screen — boredom, sex, terrible jobs, terrible bosses. Sean Penn's Jeff Spicoli became the platonic stoner-surfer template for every stoner character to come.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Aloha, Mr. Hand." — Spicoli to his strict history teacher after a long confrontation. Used as a fond goodbye to authority.
- "All I need are some tasty waves, a cool buzz, and I'm fine." — Spicoli describing his ambitions.
- "Hey bud, let's party!" — Spicoli, generic. Still the default opener at backyard barbecues among men over 45.

**Where it pops up now.** Every animated stoner character (Towelie, Shaggy in the live-action Scoobies, Otto from the Simpsons) is partly a Spicoli descendant.

## 39. [E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial (1982)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0083866/)

**Plot in plain language.** A suburban kid named Elliott befriends a stranded alien and helps him "phone home" before the government can intervene. The most successful movie of the 1980s and a cornerstone Spielberg tearjerker.

**Why it stuck.** Reese's Pieces. Bicycles in front of the moon. "Elliotttt." Everyone our age cried at this movie, full stop.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "E.T. phone home." — Said in E.T.'s gravelly voice anytime someone can't get a cell signal.
- "Be good." — E.T. pointing one glowing finger at Elliott. Used as a slightly sappy goodbye.

**Where it pops up now.** The "bike across the moon" shot is one of the most parodied images in film. Spielberg's Amblin production company *logo* is that shot.

## 40. [Risky Business (1983)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0086200/)

**Plot in plain language.** A Chicago high school senior's parents go on vacation and he turns the house into a one-weekend brothel as a get-rich scheme. Tom Cruise's launch pad.

**Why it stuck.** The shot of Cruise sliding into the living room in a button-down shirt, socks, and tighty-whities lip-syncing to Bob Seger's "Old Time Rock and Roll" is one of the most-imitated images of the decade.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Sometimes you gotta say, 'What the f***.'" — Cruise's friend Miles, delivering the movie's life philosophy. The phrase "you gotta say WTF" comes directly from this.
- "Porsche. There is no substitute." — From a chase scene.

**Where it pops up now.** Anytime anyone in any movie or commercial slides across a hardwood floor in socks, it's a Risky Business homage.

## 41. [A Christmas Story (1983)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0085334/)

**Plot in plain language.** Nine-year-old Ralphie in 1940s Indiana wants a Red Ryder BB gun for Christmas and is told repeatedly he'll shoot his eye out. Narrated by adult Ralphie looking back. TBS airs it on a 24-hour loop every Christmas Eve into Christmas Day, which is how every Gen X person has seen it 47 times whether they wanted to or not.

**Why it stuck.** It's the Christmas movie. The leg lamp, the bunny suit, the tongue stuck to the flagpole, the bullies, the Old Man's curse-cloud — every detail is a quotable bit.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "You'll shoot your eye out." — Said by Mom, the teacher, and even the department store Santa. Now the standard parental warning for any kid demanding something dangerous.
- "It's a major award!" — The Old Man receiving the leg lamp.
- "Fra-gee-lay. Must be Italian." — The Old Man reading the word "fragile" off the crate.

**Where it pops up now.** Every Christmas. Forever. The leg lamp is a real, mass-produced product now.

## 42. [Strange Brew (1983)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0086373/)

**Plot in plain language.** Bob and Doug McKenzie — two spectacularly dim Canadian brothers obsessed with beer and back bacon — get jobs at the Elsinore Brewery (yes, that Elsinore) and stumble onto an evil scheme by the brewery owner to use mind-controlled beer to take over the world. It is a loose Hamlet adaptation. Rick Moranis and Dave Thomas wrote and directed it, extending their SCTV "Great White North" sketch into a feature film.

**Why it stuck.** Bob and Doug were already cult heroes from SCTV when the film came out, and Strange Brew crystallized the characters for anyone who missed the show. "Hoser," "take off," and "beauty, eh?" all entered the vocabulary of Gen X — especially Gen X with Canadian relatives — directly from these two. The movie is proudly stupid and completely committed to it.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Take off, you hoser!" — The McKenzies' all-purpose insult. Still deployed with a bad Canadian accent when someone does something dumb.
- "Take off, eh?" — The slightly gentler version.
- "Beauty." / "Beauty, eh?" — Bob or Doug approving of literally anything. Used now as deadpan approval of something mediocre.
- "I am Brewmeister Smith. I want you to take this job and love it." — Max Von Sydow's villain, sincerely menacing. Quoted as ironic corporate-speak.

**Where it pops up now.** "Hoser" is the word for a bumbling Canadian — in any comedy, if there's a Canadian character being mocked, the Bob and Doug template is in the DNA. The movie also appears as a Hamlet parody in almost every list of Shakespeare screen adaptations, which no one expected.

## 43. [Ghostbusters (1984)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0087332/)

**Plot in plain language.** Three parapsychologists get fired from their university and start a small business catching ghosts in New York City. Then a marshmallow god threatens to destroy the city. Bill Murray smirks his way through the whole thing.

**Why it stuck.** This was THE summer-blockbuster comedy of the 80s. Every kid had a proton pack made out of a backpack and a vacuum hose. The Ray Parker Jr. theme song is still permanently lodged in our heads. The 2016 reboot, the 2021 sequel, and the merchandise just keep coming because Gen X will not let it go.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Who you gonna call?" — The theme song's hook. Used universally as "who do you call when X breaks?"
- "He slimed me." — Murray, after being ghost-slimed. Said with disgust when food spills on you.
- "Don't cross the streams." — Egon (Harold Ramis) warning against crossing the proton beams. Used metaphorically for any "don't mix two things" advice.
- "Yes, it's true. This man has no d***." — Murray on the phone, dryly. (Yes, that's the line.)
- "When someone asks if you're a god, you say YES." — Winston (Ernie Hudson) to Ray. Used as ironic life advice.

**Where it pops up now.** Every ghost-themed anything. The logo is on more t-shirts than most national flags.

## 44. [Sixteen Candles (1984)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0088128/)

**Plot in plain language.** A girl turns 16 and her whole family forgets because her sister is getting married the next day. John Hughes' first teen movie.

**Why it stuck.** Hughes invented a way of taking teenage feelings seriously while still making them funny. Molly Ringwald became the patron saint of late-80s teen-girl angst.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Donger's here for five hours and he's got somebody. I live here my whole life and I'm like a disease." — Anthony Michael Hall, complaining about romantic luck.
- "What's happenin', hot stuff?" — Long Duk Dong, one of the most-cringed-at characters in Hughes' filmography. The character is a textbook offensive Asian stereotype, and the line gets quoted with that asterisk understood.

**Where it pops up now.** Quoted with caution. The Long Duk Dong character is the standard example when people discuss what 80s teen comedies got wrong.

## 45. [This Is Spinal Tap (1984)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0088258/)

**Plot in plain language.** Fake documentary ("mockumentary") about a touring heavy-metal band that is past its prime. Rob Reiner directs and plays the filmmaker. Christopher Guest, Michael McKean, and Harry Shearer play the band. Almost entirely improvised. So many viewers thought it was a real documentary that they didn't laugh.

**Why it stuck.** It invented the mockumentary format. Everything from The Office to Curb Your Enthusiasm to every Christopher Guest movie (Best in Show, Waiting for Guffman) is built on its bones.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "These go to eleven." — Nigel Tufnel showing off an amp with a volume knob that goes one number higher than everyone else's. The phrase "goes to 11" now just means "extra intense."
- "It's such a fine line between stupid and clever." — Said in defense of basically any bad idea.
- "We've got Stonehenge!" — The band orders a Stonehenge prop and gets a tiny one due to a measurement mix-up. Used when something is comically smaller than expected.

**Where it pops up now.** "Goes to eleven" is in the actual dictionary now (OED). Every rock-doc parody descends from this.

## 46. [The Adventures of Buckaroo Banzai Across the 8th Dimension (1984)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0086856/)

**Plot in plain language.** A brain surgeon / particle physicist / rock star / samurai (Peter Weller) named Buckaroo Banzai leads a team of similarly multi-talented adventurers called the Hong Kong Cavaliers. He uses an "oscillation overthruster" to drive a rocket car *through* solid matter into the Eighth Dimension, accidentally letting through a race of evil aliens (all named "John" — John Bigboote, John Smallberries, John Yaya) led by John Lithgow as Dr. Emilio Lizardo. He has to save the world before lunch. Co-stars Jeff Goldblum in a cowboy outfit named "New Jersey," Christopher Lloyd, and Ellen Barkin. None of this is exaggeration.

**Why it stuck.** It was a complete flop in theaters and then became a *massive* cult movie on cable and VHS through the 80s. For a specific kind of Gen X nerd — the D&D / *Heavy Metal* magazine / Rush-listening crowd — this is sacred text. The deadpan commitment to making zero sense, the assumption that you already know the world, and the throwaway one-liners are why everyone who loves it loves it the same exact amount.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "No matter where you go, there you are." — Buckaroo's signature aphorism, delivered to a roomful of people listening with extreme seriousness. Used by Gen Xers any time someone is overcomplicating a simple truth. (Also the title of a Jon Kabat-Zinn meditation book, which absolutely cribbed it.)
- "Why is there a watermelon there?" / "I'll tell you later." — A Hong Kong Cavalier glancing at a watermelon in a vise on the lab table, getting brushed off, and never getting an answer. The all-time perfect line for "let's not get into it right now."
- "Laugh-a while you can, monkey-boy!" — Lithgow as Lord John Whorfin, doing a thick Italian accent inside an alien possessing a human body. Used to taunt anyone enjoying themselves a little too much.
- "Character is what you are in the dark." — Buckaroo, very earnest. Co-opted by motivational poster culture.
- The end credits promise *Buckaroo Banzai Against the World Crime League* — a sequel that never happened and has been a 40-year inside joke.

**Where it pops up now.** *Big Trouble in Little China*, *The Fifth Element*, *Galaxy Quest*, *Pacific Rim*, and most of Wes Anderson's tonal toolkit all owe it something. Any time someone confidently uses a piece of bonkers technical jargon without explaining it, that's Banzai energy. The Hong Kong Cavaliers' "cool nerds in matching jumpsuits" aesthetic gets recycled constantly — including, arguably, by half of the *Ghostbusters* lineage.

## 47. [The Karate Kid (1984)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0087538/)

**Plot in plain language.** Bullied new kid in California learns karate from his apartment building's handyman, an Okinawan immigrant named Mr. Miyagi, and enters a tournament. Sweep the leg, wax on, wax off.

**Why it stuck.** Underdog story + cool mentor + training montage = the formula. Every '80s kid did the crane kick on a coffee table.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Wax on, wax off." — Mr. Miyagi teaching Daniel the muscle memory of karate via car-waxing. Now means "this boring repetitive thing is secretly building a skill."
- "Sweep the leg, Johnny." — The villain coach Kreese ordering an illegal move. Used to mock dirty tactics.
- "Get him a body bag! Yeah!" — Cobra Kai cheering chant.

**Where it pops up now.** The Netflix series Cobra Kai is the entire franchise, alive and reanimating these lines for a new audience.

## 48. [The Breakfast Club (1985)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0088847/)

**Plot in plain language.** Five high school students from five different cliques (jock, brain, princess, basket case, criminal) spend a Saturday in detention together and have feelings. John Hughes again.

**Why it stuck.** It articulated something Gen X teens felt acutely — that high school cliques were a stupid lie and we were all the same underneath. The final freeze-frame of Judd Nelson's fist in the air, set to Simple Minds' "Don't You (Forget About Me)," is etched on the inside of every Gen X skull.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Don't you forget about me." — The song lyric, used as a (slightly dramatic) signoff.
- "Eat my shorts." — Bender (Judd Nelson) to the principal. Bart Simpson later inherited it.
- "Dear Mr. Vernon, we accept the fact that we had to sacrifice a whole Saturday in detention…" — The opening/closing letter. Sometimes quoted in full as a wedding toast. Yes, really.

**Where it pops up now.** The fist-pump-walk-off-the-field move is *the* Breakfast Club move. Any time it appears in a movie, it's a Hughes homage.

## 49. [Back to the Future (1985)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0088763/)

**Plot in plain language.** Marty McFly accidentally travels from 1985 back to 1955 in a DeLorean built by his eccentric scientist friend Doc Brown, has to get his parents to fall in love so he can exist, and then get home.

**Why it stuck.** Time-travel comedy perfection. The October 21, 2015 date that Marty travels to in the sequel became a real cultural event when that day arrived in real life.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Great Scott!" — Doc Brown's catchphrase.
- "Where we're going, we don't need roads." — Doc Brown, at the end. Used when discarding outdated tools.
- "If my calculations are correct, when this baby hits 88 miles per hour, you're gonna see some serious s***." — Doc Brown.
- "1.21 jigowatts!" — The amount of power needed to time travel. (It's actually pronounced "gigawatts," but the movie says "jigowatts," and that's how everyone says it.)
- "Why are things so heavy in the future? Is there a problem with the Earth's gravitational pull?" — Doc, hilariously.

**Where it pops up now.** Every time-travel anything references it. The DeLorean is one of the most recognizable cars in cinema.

## 50. [The Goonies (1985)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0089218/)

**Plot in plain language.** A group of kids in a Pacific Northwest town that's about to be foreclosed on find a pirate treasure map and go on an adventure through underground caves to find One-Eyed Willy's treasure.

**Why it stuck.** Foundational kids-on-bikes adventure movie. Stranger Things owes it everything.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Goonies never say die." — Mikey rallying the group. Used as a Gen X "we're not giving up" line.
- "Hey you guuuuuys!" — Sloth, the kind-hearted giant. Universally imitated, badly.

**Where it pops up now.** Stranger Things is essentially a love letter to The Goonies (and Stand By Me, and ET).

## 51. [Fletch (1985)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0089155/)

**Plot in plain language.** Investigative reporter Irwin M. "Fletch" Fletcher (Chevy Chase) goes undercover on a California beach to write about the drug trade, and a wealthy man approaches him with a bizarre offer: kill me for money, I'm dying anyway. Fletch thinks this doesn't add up and spends the rest of the movie impersonating doctors, lawyers, and a Utah Jazz player while digging into the real story.

**Why it stuck.** Chevy Chase's Fletch is the patron saint of wiseass improv. He rattles off fake names and credentials with total confidence, and the movie trusts the audience to keep up. Every Gen X person who's ever bluffed their way through something owes Fletch a royalty.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "It's all ball bearings nowadays." — Fletch confidently explaining how an engine works, knowing nothing. Used whenever someone is bluffing expertise they don't have.
- "Can I borrow your towel? My car just hit a water buffalo." — Fletch talking his way into a beach house. The delivery is the whole joke.
- "Provo. I grew up in Provo." / "Lots of good memories." — Fletch casually claiming to be from Provo to avoid questions. The name "Provo" became a Gen X shorthand for an implausibly boring backstory.
- "You've got a pretty good body for a man your age." / "Thanks. I work out." — Fletch to the man who wants him dead, completely ignoring the point of the scene.
- "Moon River..." — Fletch humming the Audrey Hepburn classic while performing a medical exam he has no business performing.

**Where it pops up now.** The structure of Fletch — wiseass journalist impersonates everyone while solving a crime — resurfaced in Jon Hamm's 2022 reboot. Every "comedic undercover reporter" character in 90s and 2000s TV owes the archetype.

## 52. [Top Gun (1986)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0092099/)

**Plot in plain language.** Hotshot Navy fighter pilot Maverick (Tom Cruise) goes to fighter weapons school. Aviator sunglasses, volleyball, romance with a flight instructor, tragic best friend death. The 2022 sequel turned out to be enormous.

**Why it stuck.** Aviator sunglasses, the bomber jacket, "I feel the need for speed," and a soundtrack that played at every roller rink. Recruitment for the U.S. Navy reportedly spiked after it came out.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "I feel the need… the need for speed!" — Maverick and Goose in unison, high-fiving. Said by Gen X dads in cars.
- "I'll be your wingman anytime." — Iceman to Maverick, the reluctant alpha-male peace offering. Used as a friendship declaration.
- "You can be my wingman anytime." — Maverick's reply.
- "Your ego is writing checks your body can't cash." — Commander to Maverick. Used as a putdown to anyone trash-talking beyond their skills.
- "Talk to me, Goose." — Maverick to his radar intercept officer. Used as "give me information, now."

**Where it pops up now.** Every aviator-shaded movie or commercial. Top Gun: Maverick (2022) was a multi-billion-dollar callback.

## 53. [Ferris Bueller's Day Off (1986)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0091042/)

**Plot in plain language.** A clever Chicago high schooler fakes being sick, takes his uptight best friend and his girlfriend on the best skip day ever — parade, museum, baseball game, fancy dinner — and outsmarts his principal and his sister. John Hughes again, again.

**Why it stuck.** Ferris Bueller is the patron saint of work-life balance for Gen X. The movie also broke the fourth wall constantly, which felt revolutionary at the time.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Life moves pretty fast. If you don't stop and look around once in a while, you could miss it." — Ferris, to the camera. The single most-quoted line in Gen X graduation speeches.
- "Bueller… Bueller… Bueller…" — Ben Stein, the deadpan economics teacher, taking attendance. Used to indicate dead silence.
- "Anyone? Anyone?" — Same teacher trying to get a class to answer. Used when nobody on a Zoom call is responding.
- "I weep for the future." — Grace, the secretary.

**Where it pops up now.** The "Bueller… Bueller…" attendance bit is referenced constantly in workplace memes about silent meetings.

## 54. [Aliens (1986)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0090605/)

**Plot in plain language.** James Cameron's sequel to Alien, set 57 years later. Sigourney Weaver's Ripley joins a squad of space marines to investigate a colony overrun by aliens. Becomes a foster mom in the process.

**Why it stuck.** Ripley is one of the all-time great action heroes — full stop, regardless of gender. The marine squad's mouthy banter set the template for every action ensemble that followed.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Get away from her, you b****!" — Ripley in the power loader, defending her foster daughter from the alien queen. The line Sigourney Weaver fans recite without prompting.
- "Game over, man! Game over!" — Hudson (Bill Paxton) panicking. The phrase "game over, man" is just how Gen X says "we're toast."
- "Nuke the site from orbit. It's the only way to be sure." — Ripley, on dealing with the alien infestation. Now a sysadmin / IT joke about wiping a server.

**Where it pops up now.** Every science fiction action movie's marines-with-pulse-rifles formula descends from this.

## 55. [The Princess Bride (1987)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0093779/)

**Plot in plain language.** A grandfather reads a fairy tale to his sick grandson — pirates, princesses, sword fights, a six-fingered villain, true love, miracles. Directed by Rob Reiner. Endlessly clever and endlessly sincere.

**Why it stuck.** It is the most-quotable single movie in Gen X history. There is functionally no Princess Bride line that isn't used regularly in conversation.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Hello. My name is Inigo Montoya. You killed my father. Prepare to die." — Inigo's revenge speech. Used in absurd contexts (a barista getting an order wrong).
- "Inconceivable!" / "You keep using that word. I do not think it means what you think it means." — Vizzini's verbal tic and Inigo's eventual correction. The "I do not think it means what you think it means" construction is now a meme template.
- "As you wish." — Westley to Buttercup, meaning "I love you." Said at the end of any agreeable conversation by Gen X spouses.
- "Have fun storming the castle!" — Miracle Max (Billy Crystal) cheerfully sending the heroes off. Used as a sarcastic well-wish before a hard task.
- "Mawage. Mawage is what bwings us togeva today." — The clergyman officiating a wedding scene, lisping. Recited at actual weddings.
- "Anybody want a peanut?" — Fezzik finishing a rhyme. Used as a non-sequitur.

**Where it pops up now.** Quoted constantly. There is a fan-screening tradition where audiences do every line out loud.

## 56. [Full Metal Jacket (1987)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0093058/)

**Plot in plain language.** Stanley Kubrick's Vietnam War movie. First half is Marine boot camp on Parris Island; second half is the war itself.

**Why it stuck.** R. Lee Ermey as the drill instructor (Gunnery Sgt. Hartman) delivered such an iconic, profane performance that the rest of the movie sometimes gets overshadowed.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- Most of Hartman's dialogue is too explicit to quote in a guide for younger readers, but the *cadence* of "what is your major malfunction" and the inventive insult structure influenced every drill-sergeant character in every comedy since.
- "Me love you long time." — Said by a character in Saigon. The line has had a complicated cultural afterlife and is now widely understood to be a slur, not a quote — handle with care.

**Where it pops up now.** The drill-sergeant rant is the template every comedy drill sergeant follows. Ermey himself did decades of voice work in that exact register.

## 57. [Predator (1987)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0093773/)

**Plot in plain language.** An elite military squad in a Central American jungle gets hunted by an invisible alien with a heat-vision mask. Arnold Schwarzenegger leads. Carl Weathers, Jesse Ventura, and a Minigun are involved.

**Why it stuck.** Maximum 80s machismo. The handshake-flex between Arnold and Carl Weathers is one of the most-memed images on the internet.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Get to the choppa!" — Arnold yelling at the survivors to reach the helicopter. Said in an Arnold voice when anyone is trying to leave somewhere.
- "I ain't got time to bleed." — Jesse Ventura's Blain. Used in fitness culture.
- "If it bleeds, we can kill it." — Arnold, philosophically.

**Where it pops up now.** Arnold's "get to the choppa" is one of the most-quoted Arnold lines, second only to maybe "I'll be back."

## 59. [The Lost Boys (1987)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0093437/)

**Plot in plain language.** Two brothers move to a California beach town and the older one falls in with a gang of vampire teenagers. The Coreys (Corey Feldman and Corey Haim) play the vampire hunters.

**Why it stuck.** It made vampires cool, hair big, and a saxophone-player on the boardwalk a national meme. Every teen-vampire property since (from Buffy to Twilight) owes it.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Sleep all day. Party all night. Never grow old. Never die. It's fun to be a vampire." — The tagline, repeated in the movie.

## 60. [Die Hard (1988)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0095016/)

**Plot in plain language.** Off-duty NYPD cop John McClane is visiting his estranged wife at her L.A. office Christmas party when European thieves take the building hostage. He spends the next two hours barefoot in an air duct.

**Why it stuck.** It invented the modern action movie (one guy in a confined space) and Bruce Willis's smirking everyman became the action-hero template for the 90s. Also: it is a Christmas movie, and Gen X will fight you on this.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Yippee-ki-yay, motherf***er." — McClane, into a radio, to the head bad guy. Now an entire vibe.
- "Welcome to the party, pal." — McClane to a cop on the ground. Used when someone finally catches up.
- "Now I have a machine gun. Ho ho ho." — McClane on a dead guy's sweater, taunting the bad guys.
- "Come out to the coast, we'll get together, have a few laughs." — McClane to himself, riding in a car.

**Where it pops up now.** Every "Die Hard in a ___" pitch (Speed is "Die Hard on a bus," Air Force One is "Die Hard on a plane") still gets greenlit because Die Hard worked.

## 63. [Heathers (1989)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0097322/)

**Plot in plain language.** A dark satire of high school cliques where a teen girl (Winona Ryder) and her sociopathic new boyfriend (Christian Slater) start murdering the popular kids and staging it as suicides.

**Why it stuck.** It was Mean Girls before Mean Girls but pitch-black. The vocabulary of the popular girls — calling everything "very" — caught on.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "What's your damage?" — Generic insult for someone being weird. Standard high school slang for years after.
- "How very." — Used to dismiss something pretentious.
- "Did you have a brain tumor for breakfast?" — Heather Chandler insult.

**Where it pops up now.** Every dark high school movie or show (Riverdale, Euphoria) owes it.

## 64. [Bill & Ted's Excellent Adventure (1989)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0096928/)

## 66. [Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade (1989)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0097576/)

**Plot in plain language.** Archaeology professor and part-time treasure hunter Indiana Jones (Harrison Ford) teams up with his stuffy, bickering father Henry Jones Sr. (Sean Connery) to find the Holy Grail before the Nazis do. Road trips through Venice, Hatay, and the Canyon of the Crescent Moon ensue.

**Why it stuck.** The father-son dynamic between Ford and Connery gave the franchise its warmest entry. It also packed in the most quotable one-liners of the trilogy, and the three challenges at the end — penitent man, name of God, leap of faith — became shorthand for puzzle-solving under pressure.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "We named the dog Indiana." — Henry Sr., casually revealing the origin of Indy's nickname. Dropped at exactly the right moment.
- "You have chosen… wisely." — The ancient Grail Knight, approving the correct chalice. Gen X uses this approvingly whenever someone makes a sound decision — ordering the right beer, picking the correct tool.
- "He chose poorly." — The Grail Knight again, watching the villain's face melt. The flip side: deployed whenever someone makes a spectacularly bad call.
- "X never, ever marks the spot." — Indy lecturing students, immediately before X marks the spot. A reliable setup-and-payback structure Gen X borrows for any situation where the rule gets broken by the person who just stated it.
- "Ah, Venice." — Henry Sr., serenely incorrect about their location. Used to name-drop the wrong city (or concept) with misplaced confidence.
- "Junior!" — Henry's preferred name for his son. Any time a parent or authority figure refuses to use the cool name.

**Where it pops up now.** "You have chosen wisely / he chose poorly" are all over sports commentary, tech-Twitter debates, and election-night reaction threads. The leap-of-faith sequence gets cited in business writing about risk and trust.

## 67. [Fletch Lives (1989)](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0097162/)

**Plot in plain language.** Irwin M. Fletcher inherits a Louisiana plantation from a great-aunt and heads south to claim it, immediately getting tangled in murder, an evangelical televangelist empire, and a toxic waste cover-up. The cultural setting shifts from L.A. beach culture to the Deep South, and Fletch spends the movie impersonating a Southern lawyer, a KKK member (briefly, and with contempt), and various other people he has no business being.

**Why it stuck.** It's the lesser Fletch, and Gen X mostly knows it as "the sequel." But it extended Fletch's run on cable and VHS through the late 80s and early 90s, and some of the Southern-fried impersonations — especially the fake-preacher material — are genuinely sharp. The Swaggart-era televangelism parody landed at exactly the right moment.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "You using the whole fist, Doc?" — Fletch, having a medical procedure, being difficult. The go-to Fletch Lives quote.
- "I'm Fletch. Irwin M. Fletcher. Chevy Chase." — No, he doesn't actually say that, but Gen X conflates the actor with the character often enough that it counts.
- "Fat Sam's — great steaks." — Fletch's fake restaurant expertise.

**Where it pops up now.** Less than the original, honestly. Mostly cited as "the one where he goes to the South." The 2022 Jon Hamm reboot drew more from the first film.
