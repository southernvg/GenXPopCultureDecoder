# The Movies of the 1990s

## 47. Goodfellas (1990)

**Plot in plain language.** Martin Scorsese's adaptation of the true story of Henry Hill, a Brooklyn kid who grew up in the mob, narrated by Hill himself. Joe Pesci, Robert De Niro, Ray Liotta.

**Why it stuck.** This is the mob movie. It's quoted in every dudes-night context, in every restaurant where waiters spend too long describing the specials, in every workplace where someone is being weasely.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Funny how? I mean funny like I'm a clown, I amuse you? I make you laugh?" — Joe Pesci as Tommy DeVito, terrifyingly pretending to take offense to being called funny. Used when someone makes a joke that lands wrong.
- "As far back as I can remember, I always wanted to be a gangster." — Henry Hill's opening narration. Quoted as the opener for any "I always wanted to be X" joke.
- "Now go home and get your shinebox." — Tommy.
- "Get the f*** out of here." — Liotta, repeatedly, with different meanings each time.

**Where it pops up now.** Every restaurant scene in cinema since 1990 is in dialogue with the Copacabana steadicam tracking shot.

## 48. Home Alone (1990)

**Plot in plain language.** A Chicago family forgets their 8-year-old son Kevin at home when they leave for Christmas in Paris, and Kevin booby-traps the house to stop two burglars. The McCallisters are confirmed wealthy because that house. John Hughes (writer) and Chris Columbus (director).

**Why it stuck.** Every Gen X-er and Millennial saw it on VHS approximately 400 times. The booby-trap sequence is a national treasure.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Keep the change, ya filthy animal." — The line Kevin plays on his TV from a fake old gangster movie to scare delivery people. Universally quoted at Christmas.
- "AAAAAAAAHHH!" — The hands-on-face Macaulay Culkin scream. The Home Alone poster image.
- "I made my family disappear." — Kevin, jubilantly.

**Where it pops up now.** Every Christmas movie list, every meme about being left alone over the holidays.

## 49. Wayne's World (1992)

**Plot in plain language.** An expansion of the Saturday Night Live sketch — two metalhead public-access TV hosts (Mike Myers and Dana Carvey) from Aurora, Illinois, accidentally sell their show to a slick TV producer and have to take it back.

**Why it stuck.** "Schwing," "we're not worthy," and the head-banging Bohemian Rhapsody car scene became the entire 90s in 30 seconds.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Party on, Wayne. Party on, Garth." — Their signoff.
- "Schwing!" — The sound Wayne makes when an attractive woman walks by. (Yes, the joke has aged.)
- "We're not worthy!" — Wayne and Garth bowing to Alice Cooper backstage. Used as comic flattery.
- "Excellent." — Wayne's verdict on anything good. Mr. Burns from the Simpsons made the same word famous in a different register.
- "Game on!" — Yelled when playing street hockey in front of their house. Used to start anything competitive.

**Where it pops up now.** The Bohemian Rhapsody car scene is one of the most-imitated bits in movie history. Anyone in their 40s has done it.

## 50. Sneakers (1992)

**Plot in plain language.** A team of professional security testers — they break into banks and offices for a living to find weaknesses — gets blackmailed by what looks like the NSA into stealing a mysterious black box. The box turns out to be a universal codebreaker that can crack any encrypted system on Earth. The crew (Robert Redford, Sidney Poitier, Dan Aykroyd, River Phoenix, David Strathairn as a blind sound expert, Mary McDonnell) has to get it back from a villain played by Ben Kingsley, who happens to be Redford's college friend from a 1960s hacking scheme gone wrong. Smart, funny, low-key — a heist movie disguised as a tech thriller.

**Why it stuck.** *Sneakers* is the patron saint of the technically-literate Gen X workplace. Anyone who works in security, IT, infosec, or anywhere near a server room has watched it more times than they will admit. It got the *feel* of hacker culture right — the curiosity, the ethics arguments, the obsession with payphones and modems — at exactly the moment the internet was about to go mainstream. It also has one of the best ensembles of the 90s and a tight, witty script.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Setec Astronomy." — The cryptic phrase printed on the black box. Eventually decoded as an anagram for "Too Many Secrets." Used as a knowing nod whenever something is being hidden in plain sight.
- "Too many secrets." — Said in the same hushed Redford tone whenever a Gen X person is alluding to an unexplained company decision.
- "My voice is my passport. Verify me." — The biometric voice-print sample. Quoted by every infosec professional on Earth when explaining why voiceprint auth is not a great idea.
- "There's a war out there, old friend. A world war. And it's not about who's got the most bullets. It's about who controls the information." — Kingsley's villain monologue. Now wildly more relevant than it was in 1992. Quoted with a sigh.
- "Cattle mutilations are up." — Dan Aykroyd's character explaining his current conspiracy obsession. The all-purpose Gen X line for "let me tell you what's actually going on."

**Where it pops up now.** Every "ethical hacker" thriller (*Mr. Robot*, the *Ocean's* movies' tech beats, *Leverage*) is a *Sneakers* descendant. The "too many secrets" / "who controls the information" speeches get quoted in essays about big tech, surveillance capitalism, and AI roughly every other week. The whole genre of "rumpled experts in a van full of monitors" — that's this movie.

## 51. A Few Good Men (1992)

**Plot in plain language.** Aaron Sorkin courtroom drama. A Navy lawyer (Tom Cruise) defends two Marines accused of killing a fellow Marine in a hazing ritual. Jack Nicholson plays the base commander who ordered it.

**Why it stuck.** The climactic cross-examination is a top-three movie courtroom scene. Nicholson chewing scenery is at its absolute peak.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "You can't handle the truth!" — Nicholson, finally exploding under questioning. Used in any argument where someone is being told something hard.
- "You want me on that wall. You need me on that wall." — Nicholson's rationalization speech. Quoted with a half-mocking, half-serious tone.

**Where it pops up now.** "You can't handle the truth" is a top-five courtroom line ever. It is in commercials for everything.

## 52. Reservoir Dogs (1992)

**Plot in plain language.** Quentin Tarantino's debut. A diamond heist goes wrong; the surviving crooks meet in a warehouse and try to figure out who the rat is. Color-coded code names (Mr. Pink, Mr. White, Mr. Orange, etc.).

**Why it stuck.** It launched Tarantino and the 90s indie film boom. The opening diner scene of guys debating Madonna lyrics and whether to tip became the template for every "guys arguing about pop culture" scene.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Are you gonna bark all day, little doggie, or are you gonna bite?" — Mr. Blonde.
- The Mr. Pink "I don't tip" speech is quoted at any group meal where tipping comes up.

**Where it pops up now.** The walking-in-slow-motion-in-suits-and-sunglasses shot is the most-parodied image in 90s movies.

## 53. Aladdin (1992)

**Plot in plain language.** Disney animated take on the Arabian Nights tale. The genie, voiced by Robin Williams, steals the entire movie. Williams improvised so much that the Academy disqualified it from the writing categories.

**Why it stuck.** The genie's pop-culture-reference machine-gun delivery was a kid's introduction to a hundred different impressions. The "A Whole New World" magic-carpet song was every elementary-school sing-along.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "PHENOMENAL COSMIC POWERS! Itty bitty living space!" — The genie, after Aladdin frees him to enter a tiny lamp.
- "Ten thousand years will give you such a crick in the neck!"

**Where it pops up now.** Robin Williams' performance is the reference point for every modern animated comedy sidekick voice work.

## 54. Wind (1992)

**Plot in plain language.** A young sailor (Matthew Modine) blows his shot at winning the America's Cup for the United States, and the boat goes to Australia. Disgraced, he goes off to the desert with his ex-girlfriend (Jennifer Grey) and a brilliant aerospace engineer (Stellan Skarsgård) to design a radical new boat, recruit a ragtag crew, and challenge the Australians to take the Cup back. Directed by Carroll Ballard (*The Black Stallion*, *Never Cry Wolf*), who knew how to shoot wind and water like no one else. The on-water cinematography is still some of the best ever filmed.

**Why it stuck.** This is a deep cut, with a very specific fanbase. If your Gen X friend sails — or grew up around boats, or lived near a marina, or just had one uncle who watched it on cable until the tape wore out — they have seen *Wind* dozens of times and they will recite chunks of it at you on the dock. It is also a quietly perfect underdog/redemption sports movie that simply happens to be about sailing instead of football. Most younger people have never heard of it; everyone who's seen it remembers it.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Why don't we just build a faster boat?" — Skarsgård's engineer, deadpan, reframing the entire problem. The all-purpose Gen X line for "stop optimizing the wrong thing and go fix the actual issue."
- "It's just wind." — Modine's character on the way out to a race. Said by sailors when something is being made into a bigger deal than it needs to be.
- "Sail the boat, son. Don't let the boat sail you." — The grizzled mentor line. Used as life advice by sailing dads everywhere.

**Where it pops up now.** Every modern sailing documentary (the *Untold* America's Cup episodes, anything about Team New Zealand or Oracle) leans on the visual language Ballard set up here. If you ever hear a 50-year-old at a yacht club or lakeside bar say "we just need to build a faster boat," they're not being original — they're quoting *Wind*. The film also helped seed a generation of engineers and naval architects; ask any of them what got them into sailing and a surprising number will name this movie.

## 55. So I Married an Axe Murderer (1993)

**Plot in plain language.** A San Francisco poet (Mike Myers) suspects his new wife (Nancy Travis) is a serial killer. Mike Myers also plays his own dad in a brilliant Scottish bit.

**Why it stuck.** A cult favorite. Every Gen X person has done the "head" line in a bad Scottish accent at least once.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "HEED! Move!" / "It's like an orange, on a toothpick!" — Mike Myers' Scottish dad on the size of his older son's head. Said at every family dinner forever.
- "Excuse me, miss, there's a hair in my coffee."

**Where it pops up now.** Big-headed family members get this quoted at them constantly.

## 56. Groundhog Day (1993)

**Plot in plain language.** Cynical TV weatherman Phil Connors (Bill Murray) gets stuck living the same February 2nd in Punxsutawney, PA, over and over until he becomes a better person. Directed by Harold Ramis.

**Why it stuck.** It's an actual philosophical text now — referenced in self-help, religion, behavioral science, and Buddhism classes — about how you'd live if every day were the same.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Well, what if there is no tomorrow? There wasn't one today."
- "Don't drive angry!" — Phil yelling at the actual groundhog who is driving a stolen truck.
- "I'm a god. I'm not THE God… I don't think." — Phil, fed up.
- "It's gonna be cold, it's gonna be gray, and it's gonna last you for the rest of your life."

**Where it pops up now.** Any time anyone says "it's like Groundhog Day around here" they mean a repeating bad day, and they got that from this. Boomers do this too but they got it from us.

## 57. Jurassic Park (1993)

**Plot in plain language.** Spielberg adapts Michael Crichton. A rich guy clones dinosaurs for a theme park, things go wrong, Jeff Goldblum gets to deliver several great speeches about chaos theory.

**Why it stuck.** The T-Rex paddock scene with the cup of water rippling, the kitchen velociraptors, Sam Neill's hat. Foundational summer blockbuster.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Your scientists were so preoccupied with whether they could, they didn't stop to think if they should." — Ian Malcolm (Goldblum). Used about every new technology — most recently AI.
- "Life, uh, finds a way." — Ian Malcolm again. Used to describe any unstoppable process.
- "Hold onto your butts." — Samuel L. Jackson before flipping the power back on. Universal "this is gonna be wild" line.
- "Clever girl." — Robert Muldoon, the game warden, realizing the raptor outflanked him.

**Where it pops up now.** Every science movie quotes Ian Malcolm. "Life, uh, finds a way" is in policy debates.

## 58. The Sandlot (1993)

**Plot in plain language.** A bookish new kid in a 1960s suburban neighborhood gets adopted by a group of baseball-playing kids and they spend a summer trying to retrieve a Babe Ruth-signed ball from the giant dog ("The Beast") behind the field's fence.

**Why it stuck.** Quotable summer nostalgia for an era before screens. Every Little League team has done at least one Sandlot line.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "You're killin' me, Smalls!" — Ham (the chubby catcher) at Smalls (the new kid). The single most-quoted line in dad culture. Said by every father to every confused son.
- "You play ball like a girl!" — The neighborhood rival kids' insult, delivered as the ultimate burn. (Now used ironically/jokingly given how it's aged.)
- "For-EV-er. For-EV-er. For-EV-er." — Squints describing how long the ball will be lost to the Beast.
- "Heroes get remembered, but legends never die."

**Where it pops up now. "**You're killin' me, Smalls" is now its own meme template entirely independent of the movie.

## 59. Dazed and Confused (1993)

**Plot in plain language.** Richard Linklater's hangout movie set on the last day of high school in 1976 in Texas. Matthew McConaughey's launch pad.

**Why it stuck.** It captured a specific mid-70s vibe so well that 90s teens watched it to imagine they were 70s teens. McConaughey's Wooderson is one of the most-quoted characters of the decade.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Alright, alright, alright." — Wooderson's intro line. McConaughey now opens every Oscar speech with it.
- "That's what I love about these high school girls, man. I get older, they stay the same age." — Wooderson, in a line that has aged… let's say poorly. But the alright-alright-alright is the part everyone repeats.

**Where it pops up now.** McConaughey's entire career has been a victory lap on this performance.

## 60. Forrest Gump (1994)

**Plot in plain language.** A slow-witted but extraordinarily kind man from Alabama (Tom Hanks) accidentally Zelig-style witnesses every major event in late-20th-century American history. Bubba Gump shrimp company. Lt. Dan. Jenny.

**Why it stuck.** Hanks' performance and the line "life is like a box of chocolates" went mainstream so fast that the movie was already a meme by Christmas of 1994.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Mama always said, life is like a box of chocolates. You never know what you're gonna get." — Forrest, on the bench. The defining line.
- "Stupid is as stupid does." — Forrest, when called stupid. Used to dismiss insults.
- "Run, Forrest, run!" — Yelled at any jogger by any group of teens with bad manners. Sorry, joggers.
- "And just like that… ___." — The narration structure has become its own meme template.

**Where it pops up now.** Every life-spans-decades story film is in conversation with Forrest Gump.

## 61. Pulp Fiction (1994)

**Plot in plain language.** Tarantino's interwoven crime stories in 1990s Los Angeles. Two hitmen (Samuel L. Jackson and John Travolta), a boxer (Bruce Willis), a gangster's wife (Uma Thurman), a diner robbery, and a briefcase full of mystery.

**Why it stuck.** It changed indie filmmaking. The dance scene at Jack Rabbit Slim's. The "Royale with Cheese" conversation. Every aspiring screenwriter studied this movie for the next decade.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Say 'what' again. Say 'what' again, I dare you, I double-dare you, motherf***er!" — Jules (Sam Jackson) interrogating a kid in an apartment. The setup for the Ezekiel 25:17 speech.
- "Royale with Cheese." — Vincent explaining what a Quarter Pounder is called in France. Said any time anyone discusses fast food in any country.
- "I'm gonna get medieval on your a**." — Marsellus Wallace, after a horrible incident. Used as a threat.
- "Zed's dead, baby. Zed's dead."
- "English, motherf***er, do you speak it?" — Jules.

**Where it pops up now.** Every interlocking-stories film, every diner conversation about pop culture, every Sam Jackson role descends from this.

## 62. Dumb and Dumber (1994)

**Plot in plain language.** Two extremely dumb best friends (Jim Carrey and Jeff Daniels) drive across the country to return a briefcase to a woman one of them has fallen for. They do not realize it's a ransom payment.

**Why it stuck.** Jim Carrey's peak era. The chipped tooth, the haircut, the orange and blue tuxedos.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "So you're telling me there's a chance!" — Lloyd, after being told the odds of dating his crush are one in a million. Used by every optimist with bad data.
- "Big Gulps, huh? Welp, see ya later!" — Harry, at a 7-Eleven, awkwardly leaving a conversation.
- "We landed on the moon!"
- "Yeah, I called her up. She gave me a bunch of crap about me not listening to her, or something. I don't know, I wasn't really paying attention."

**Where it pops up now.** "So you're telling me there's a chance" is in basically every romantic-comedy parody.

## 63. Clerks (1994)

**Plot in plain language.** Kevin Smith's black-and-white debut, shot in the convenience store he was working at. Two slacker clerks (Dante and Randal) spend a day arguing, dealing with terrible customers, and rambling about Star Wars.

**Why it stuck.** It was the first movie that captured the specific tone of Gen X retail-worker resentment. The "I'm not even supposed to be here today" line is the slacker national anthem.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "I'm not even supposed to be here today!" — Dante, repeatedly. Used by every retail worker, ever.
- "Try not to suck any d*** on the way to the parking lot!" — Randal yelling at a customer. (Yes, that's the line.)
- The discussion of whether the contractors who built the Death Star were innocent civilians is a foundational Gen X bull-session text.

**Where it pops up now.** Every workplace comedy from this point forward — Office Space, Empire Records, The Office — descends partly from Clerks.

## 64. The Lion King (1994)

**Plot in plain language.** Disney animated Hamlet with lions. Simba's dad gets killed by his uncle, Simba runs away, meets a warthog and a meerkat, has to come back and reclaim the throne.

**Why it stuck.** "Hakuna Matata" is now a real phrase in English. The opening "Circle of Life" sequence is its own visual shorthand.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Hakuna Matata. It means no worries." — Timon and Pumbaa.
- "Long live the king." — Scar, dropping Mufasa.

**Where it pops up now.** Every Disney remake. Beyoncé's 2019 version. "Hakuna Matata" is now functional vocabulary.

## 65. Friday (1995)

**Plot in plain language.** Ice Cube and Chris Tucker hang out on a porch in South Central L.A. on a Friday and a lot of things happen — drug dealers, sketchy neighbors, a bully named Deebo.

**Why it stuck.** Chris Tucker's manic energy. The porch as a setting for everything. "Bye Felicia" becoming a permanent piece of slang.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Bye, Felisha." — Ice Cube dismissing an annoying acquaintance. Now spelled "Bye Felicia" and used everywhere to dismiss anyone.
- "You got knocked the f*** out!" — Used in playful trash talk after any kind of loss.
- "Da*n!" — Smokey (Chris Tucker), drawn out. Imitated badly by everyone.

**Where it pops up now.** "Bye Felicia" is the line that escaped the movie and went mainstream forever.

## 66. Tommy Boy (1995)

**Plot in plain language.** Chris Farley plays the dim-witted heir to his late father's auto-parts company; he hits the road with a sarcastic accountant (David Spade) to save the family business from being bought out. Farley falls down a lot.

**Why it stuck.** The Farley-Spade chemistry plus Farley's physical comedy peak. It is the best sustained Chris Farley vehicle and Gen X men quote it constantly.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Holy schnikes!" — Tommy's go-to exclamation.
- "Fat guy in a little coat." — Farley wearing Spade's tiny jacket and tearing it, singing his own song. Performed at every middle-school dance and bachelor party.
- "Brothers don't shake hands, brothers gotta hug!" — Tommy to his "brother."
- "I can get a good look at a T-bone steak by sticking my head up a bull's a**, but I'd rather take a butcher's word for it." — Tommy, mid-pitch, hilariously.
- "Did you eat a lot of paint chips when you were a kid?"

**Where it pops up now.** "Fat guy in a little coat" is a standalone meme.

## 67. Billy Madison (1995)

**Plot in plain language.** Adam Sandler plays a grown man who has to repeat grades 1-12 in a single year to inherit his father's hotel chain. Idiotic. Wonderful.

**Why it stuck.** It was the first Sandler movie. Set up the entire "Sandler comedy" formula that ran for the next 25 years.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "O'Doyle rules!" — The chant of a bullying family of brothers, all named O'Doyle, at every grade level. Used jokingly when any family or team is dominant.
- "What you've just said is one of the most insanely idiotic things I have ever heard. At no point in your rambling, incoherent response were you even close to anything that could be considered a rational thought…" — The academic decathlon moderator, used by Gen X writers when grading internet arguments.
- "T-t-today, junior!" — Sandler to a kid in dodgeball.
- "Stop looking at me, swan!"

**Where it pops up now.** Every Sandler movie since basically rhymes with this. The "rambling incoherent response" speech is the all-time best comeback for online debates.

## 68. Apollo 13 (1995)

**Plot in plain language.** Ron Howard recreates the real 1970 mission where an oxygen tank exploded on the way to the moon and NASA had to figure out how to get the astronauts home. Tom Hanks, Kevin Bacon, Bill Paxton, Ed Harris.

**Why it stuck.** Tense, beautifully made, true. And the lines stuck.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Houston, we have a problem." — Often misquoted (the real line is "Houston, we've had a problem"). Used in every "uh oh" moment in every office in America.
- "Failure is not an option." — Ed Harris as flight director Gene Kranz, rallying the team. Now mandatory on motivational posters.

**Where it pops up now.** "Houston, we have a problem" is one of the top-five most-quoted movie lines in existence.

## 69. Hackers (1995)

**Plot in plain language.** A group of New York City teenage hackers — Jonny Lee Miller as "Zero Cool" / Crash Override, Angelina Jolie as Acid Burn, Matthew Lillard as Cereal Killer, and friends — get framed by a corporate security villain ("The Plague") who is running a real crime through the same systems they've been goofing around in. They have to clear their names by out-hacking him, mostly by rollerblading around Manhattan and typing very fast at brightly colored screens. The actual computer stuff in the movie is, generously, *aspirational*. The aesthetic is a 1995 club kid's idea of what cyberspace will look like.

**Why it stuck.** *Hackers* failed in theaters and immediately became a cult movie on VHS, then a permanent fixture once the actual hacker community discovered it and adopted it ironically and then sincerely. It's the patron movie of computer-science-major dorm rooms. The soundtrack (Prodigy, Orbital, Underworld, Leftfield, Massive Attack) is also a perfect snapshot of 1995 electronica. And it's where most people first saw a teenage Angelina Jolie.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Hack the planet!" — Cereal Killer's rallying cry, screamed at the camera. Has been printed on a thousand t-shirts. Said unironically at every DEF CON.
- "Mess with the best, die like the rest." — The villain's smug calling card. Used as a trash-talk line in any competitive context, especially over a video game.
- "RISC architecture is gonna change everything." — Cereal Killer at a computer store, said with maximum conviction. Used to mock anyone making a big confident prediction about tech.
- "There is no right and wrong. There's only fun and boring." — Acid Burn explaining her ethics. Quoted whenever a Gen Xer wants to justify a questionable decision.

**Where it pops up now.** Every "neon-soaked cyberpunk hacker kid" aesthetic — *Mr. Robot* (in its softer moments), the Cyberpunk 2077 marketing, half of TikTok's "hacker aesthetic" tag — descends from this movie. "Hack the planet" is now a real shorthand in cybersecurity for ambitious-but-naive ambition. Anyone who works in security under the age of 55 has the movie's poster, the soundtrack, or both.

## 70. Toy Story (1995)

**Plot in plain language.** Pixar's first feature. Toys come alive when their owner isn't watching. Woody the cowboy and Buzz Lightyear the spaceman fight, then become friends.

**Why it stuck.** It changed animation forever and gave every kid the suspicion that their toys had a secret life.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "To infinity… and beyond!" — Buzz Lightyear's catchphrase. Said by every Gen X parent reading bedtime stories.
- "You've got a friend in me." — The Randy Newman song. Now standard friend-anniversary card material.

**Where it pops up now.** Every Pixar movie. Every meme of toys looking betrayed.

## 71. Happy Gilmore (1996)

**Plot in plain language.** A failed hockey player (Adam Sandler) discovers he can hit a golf ball 400 yards with a hockey-style slap shot and joins the PGA tour to win money to save his grandma's house.

**Why it stuck.** Sandler at the peak of his rage-comedy era. Bob Barker fight. Shooter McGavin's chip-on-shoulder villain. The 2025 sequel made it a generational event again.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "The price is wrong, b****!" — Happy fighting Bob Barker on a golf course. Quoted any time anyone plays The Price is Right.
- "Just tap it in. Give it a little tappy. Tap-tap-taparoo." — Happy talking to his golf ball.
- "You eat pieces of s*** for breakfast?" / "NO!" / "You do." — Pre-fight insult exchange.
- "Are you too good for your home?!" — Happy yelling at his golf ball.
- "I eat pieces of s*** like you for breakfast." / "You eat pieces of s*** for breakfast?" — Shooter McGavin and Happy.

**Where it pops up now.** Every televised golf event eventually invites the comparison. Sandler put the sequel on Netflix in 2025 and the meme cycle started again.

## 72. Trainspotting (1996)

**Plot in plain language.** Danny Boyle's grimy, kinetic adaptation of the Irvine Welsh novel about Edinburgh heroin addicts trying (or not) to get clean. Ewan McGregor's launch pad.

**Why it stuck.** The opening "Choose Life" monologue is one of the most famous voice-overs in 90s cinema.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Choose life. Choose a job. Choose a career. Choose a family. Choose a f***ing big television…" — Renton's opening monologue, used as a sardonic anti-consumerism rant template.

**Where it pops up now.** Every "Choose ___" parody monologue is from this.

## 73. Jerry Maguire (1996)

**Plot in plain language.** Tom Cruise as a sports agent who has a moral crisis, writes a mission statement, gets fired, and tries to build a new agency with one client (Cuba Gooding Jr.) and one secretary (Renée Zellweger). Cameron Crowe, third appearance.

**Why it stuck.** Three of the most-quoted lines of the decade are all in this one movie.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Show me the money!" — Cuba Gooding Jr.'s receiver Rod Tidwell making Cruise yell it on the phone. Used at every salary negotiation joke.
- "You had me at hello." — Renée Zellweger's Dorothy, ending the climactic speech. Sappy and now ironic.
- "You complete me." — Cruise to Zellweger. Standard wedding-toast/parody fodder.

**Where it pops up now.** "Show me the money" is in literally every salary conversation. Every romantic comedy quotes "you had me at hello."

## 74. Fargo (1996)

**Plot in plain language.** Coen brothers' Minnesota crime story. A car salesman (William H. Macy) hires two thugs to kidnap his wife so his rich father-in-law will pay ransom; things go very wrong. Frances McDormand as the very pregnant, very polite police chief.

**Why it stuck.** The accents. Marge Gunderson. The woodchipper.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Yah, you betcha." — Marge's catchphrase. Used to mock Midwestern politeness.
- "I'm not sure I agree with you a hundred percent on your police work, there, Lou."
- "Oh geez." — Used in every Coen brothers-flavored impression.

**Where it pops up now.** The FX TV series Fargo (multiple seasons, ongoing) keeps the spirit alive.

## 75. Swingers (1996)

**Plot in plain language.** A heartbroken aspiring actor (Jon Favreau) in mid-90s LA tries to get over his ex with the help of his motormouth friend Trent (Vince Vaughn). They drive to Vegas. They go to swing clubs.

**Why it stuck.** The phrase "you're so money and you don't even know it" became the actual way young men talked for about 18 months in 1997.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "You're so money and you don't even know it." — Trent to Mike, pumping him up.
- "Vegas, baby. Vegas." — Said by every Gen X male before any Vegas trip.

**Where it pops up now.** Jon Favreau directing Iron Man and the entire MCU starts here. So you actually have heard this movie's influence even if you've never seen it.

## 76. Austin Powers: International Man of Mystery (1997)

**Plot in plain language.** Mike Myers as a swinging-60s British secret agent thawed out in 1997 to fight Dr. Evil, also played by Myers. Spoof of every Bond convention.

**Why it stuck.** Catchphrase factory. "Yeah, baby, yeah!" is on every birthday card.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Yeah, baby, yeah!" — Austin's catchphrase.
- "Do I make you horny, baby?" — Austin.
- "One million dollars." — Dr. Evil, with the pinky to the lip, in a billion-dollar plot. Used by anyone overestimating the value of something.
- "Sharks with frickin' laser beams attached to their heads." — Dr. Evil.
- "Throw me a frickin' bone here!" — Dr. Evil pleading.

**Where it pops up now.** Every spy spoof descends from this. Dr. Evil's pinky-to-mouth is one of the all-time gesture-memes.

## 77. Boogie Nights (1997)

**Plot in plain language.** Paul Thomas Anderson's epic about the late-70s/early-80s San Fernando Valley adult-film industry, centered on a young man named Eddie Adams who becomes "Dirk Diggler." Mark Wahlberg, Burt Reynolds, Julianne Moore, John C. Reilly.

**Why it stuck.** The needle-drops, the long tracking shots, the cocaine-fueled climax. It's also where John C. Reilly transformed into a leading comic actor.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- Mostly quoted via tone and dance moves rather than specific phrases. Hard to extract a single line that's safe for school.

## 78. Titanic (1997)

**Plot in plain language.** James Cameron's three-hour blockbuster. A poor artist and a rich passenger fall in love aboard a doomed cruise liner.

**Why it stuck.** Biggest movie of the 90s. Every middle-school girl saw it three times in theaters. Every middle-school boy was forced to.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "I'm the king of the world!" — Jack on the bow. Said by every Gen X-er at the top of a hill.
- "Draw me like one of your French girls." — Rose to Jack.
- "I'll never let go, Jack. I'll never let go." — Rose, then immediately letting go. The single most-debated movie ending of the decade — there was room on that door.

**Where it pops up now.** The "there was room on the door" debate is still ongoing. The Celine Dion song is at every middle school dance forever.

## 79. Good Will Hunting (1997)

**Plot in plain language.** A genius MIT janitor (Matt Damon) gets recruited by a math professor and put in therapy with a townie therapist (Robin Williams) after a fight gets him in legal trouble. Damon and Ben Affleck wrote it; Williams won the Oscar.

**Why it stuck.** Williams' "it's not your fault" scene is one of the great emotional gut-punches in 90s cinema.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "How do you like them apples?" — Will (Damon) to a snooty Harvard kid after embarrassing him. Used as the universal mic-drop line.
- "It's not your fault." — Williams to Damon, repeated until Will breaks down. Quoted seriously and parodically.
- "My boy's wicked smaht."

**Where it pops up now.** Every "townie genius" trope is in dialogue with this. The Ben Affleck "best part of my day" speech gets used in best-man speeches.

## 80. The Big Lebowski (1998)

**Plot in plain language.** Coen brothers' shaggy Los Angeles noir. A bowling alley regular known as "The Dude" (Jeff Bridges) gets mistaken for a millionaire of the same name, his rug gets ruined, and a kidnapping plot pulls him deeper. John Goodman as Walter, an unhinged Vietnam vet. Steve Buscemi as Donny.

**Why it stuck.** The Dude is a Gen X icon — laid-back, bathrobe, White Russian in hand. There's a religion (Dudeism), an annual festival (Lebowski Fest), and a thousand t-shirts.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "The Dude abides." — The Dude's signoff. The motto.
- "That rug really tied the room together." — The Dude. Said any time anyone redecorates.
- "Yeah, well, that's just, like, your opinion, man." — The Dude. The supreme Gen X eyeroll.
- "You're not wrong, Walter. You're just an a**hole."
- "I'm the Dude. So that's what you call me, you know? That, or, uh, His Dudeness, or uh, Duder, or El Duderino if you're not into the whole brevity thing."
- "Shut the f*** up, Donny." — Walter, repeatedly, to Donny. Universal way to tell a friend to stop interrupting.

**Where it pops up now.** The Dude's whole vibe — including the rug, the bowling, the White Russian — is the Halloween costume of choice for any Gen X-er trying to seem chill.

## 81. There's Something About Mary (1998)

**Plot in plain language.** A nerdy guy (Ben Stiller) hires a private investigator (Matt Dillon) to track down his high-school crush (Cameron Diaz); the investigator falls for her too. The Farrelly Brothers' gross-out classic.

**Why it stuck.** The most-quoted gross-out comedy of the late 90s. Several scenes are too explicit to summarize here.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "I work with retards." — A character claiming his job, badly. The line has not aged well — handle with care.
- "Franks and beans! Franks and beans!" — A pet name your grandparents might call breakfast that sets up a horrible visual gag.

**Where it pops up now.** Every R-rated comedy that came after — American Pie, Wedding Crashers, the rest of the Farrelly catalog — owes this movie.

## 82. Half Baked (1998)

**Plot in plain language.** Dave Chappelle's first vehicle. A trio of stoner roommates have to sell weed to bail out their friend who accidentally killed a police horse by feeding it diabetic candy.

**Why it stuck.** Stoner-comedy cult classic. Every Gen X stoner can recite multiple bits.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "I want to talk to Samson!" — The horse owner sobbing about her dead horse.
- "Killer's a buddy of mine. He's right here." — Thurgood (Chappelle) lying to a tough character.
- "You ever look at the back of a twenty-dollar bill… on weed?" — Scarface, stoned, before the camera pulls back to reveal everyone is stoned.
- "Sir Smoke-a-Lot." — One of the stoner nicknames.

**Where it pops up now.** Every modern stoner comedy quotes it. Chappelle himself references it on his stand-up specials.

## 83. The Waterboy (1998)

**Plot in plain language.** Sandler as a sheltered, mama's-boy, water-delivery kid for a college football team who turns out to have an amazing tackling rage and becomes a linebacker.

**Why it stuck.** Sandler's southern-accent character, the "mama said" running joke, Henry Winkler as the coach.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Mama said ____ was the devil!" — Bobby Boucher quoting his overprotective mother on everything (foosball, school, women, alligators). Mad-libs structure used constantly.
- "Now that's high quality H2O." — Bobby on his water.
- "You can do it!" — Rob Schneider's Cajun. Said in a thick Cajun voice to anyone trying to do anything.

**Where it pops up now.** The "you can do it!" Schneider bit became a Sandler-movie running joke that lasted across films.

## 84. Rushmore (1998)

**Plot in plain language.** Wes Anderson's breakout. A pretentious 15-year-old prep-school student (Jason Schwartzman) befriends a depressed industrialist (Bill Murray) and they fall for the same teacher.

**Why it stuck.** Wes Anderson's whole career starts here. Murray's late-career renaissance starts here.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Take dead aim on the rich boys. Get them in the crosshairs. And take them down." — Murray's Herman Blume, advising kids at chapel.
- "Sic transit gloria. Glory fades."

## 85. Office Space (1999)

**Plot in plain language.** A demoralized programmer at a tech company (Ron Livingston) gets hypnotized into a state of total disengagement, stops trying at work, becomes more successful as a result, and plots a small-scale embezzlement scheme with two coworkers. Mike Judge (Beavis and Butt-Head, King of the Hill) directs.

**Why it stuck.** Every white-collar worker since 1999 has felt seen by this movie. The TPS reports. The flair. The Swingline stapler. The printer.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "Yeahhh, if you could go ahead and ____ that'd be greaaaat." — The boss Lumbergh's passive-aggressive request format. The single most-mimicked manager voice in office culture.
- "I believe you have my stapler." — Milton, the quiet coworker whose stapler keeps getting taken.
- "It's not that I'm lazy, it's that I just don't care." — Peter.
- "Looks like somebody's got a case of the Mondays." — A perky coworker. Said sarcastically by every office worker ever.
- "PC LOAD LETTER. What the f*** does that mean?" — Looking at a printer error.

**Where it pops up now.** Almost every workplace meme on the internet — Bob the Builder asking "what would you say you do here," the printer-smashing scene as a stress-relief reference, the red stapler — is from this movie.

## 86. The Matrix (1999)

**Plot in plain language.** A hacker named Neo (Keanu Reeves) discovers reality is a computer simulation and that humanity is enslaved by machines using us as batteries. Bullet-time fights. Sunglasses. Trench coats. The Wachowskis directing.

**Why it stuck.** It changed action-movie aesthetics. Slow-motion bullet dodging became a parodied effect for the next 20 years. "Red pill" and "blue pill" became real terminology used (and misused) in politics.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "There is no spoon." — A child psychic to Neo. Used as a mind-bend reminder.
- "I know kung fu." — Neo, after downloading martial arts.
- "Whoa." — Keanu, again, multiple times.
- "Red pill or blue pill?" — Morpheus offering Neo a choice. The phrase has unfortunately been co-opted by online subcultures, but its original meaning was simply "comfortable illusion or harsh reality."

**Where it pops up now.** Every "what if reality isn't real" plot, from Inception onward. The "red pill" terminology has its own complicated cultural afterlife.

---

## 87. Fight Club (1999)

**Plot in plain language.** A nameless insomniac office worker (Edward Norton) meets a soap salesman (Brad Pitt) and they start an underground fighting club that becomes a movement. Twist ending.

**Why it stuck.** Late-90s masculinity-crisis cinema. The Tyler Durden monologues launched a thousand college dorm-room arguments.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "The first rule of Fight Club is: you do not talk about Fight Club." — Tyler Durden's rules. Endlessly mad-libbed: "the first rule of _____ club is…"
- "I am Jack's ____ ____." — The narrator naming his own emotions. Used as a melodramatic self-narration joke.

**Where it pops up now.** "First rule of ____ club" is one of the most reused sentence templates on the internet.

## 88. American Pie (1999)

**Plot in plain language.** Four high school senior boys make a pact to lose their virginity by prom night. Eugene Levy plays the dad who keeps giving inappropriately frank advice. Jason Biggs plays a character with a now-infamous pie-related scene.

**Why it stuck.** It started the late-90s and 2000s teen-sex-comedy boom (Road Trip, EuroTrip, etc.). Stifler's mom became a meme.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "This one time, at band camp…" — Michelle the flute player's repeated story opener. Used to mock anyone telling a long boring anecdote.
- "MILF." — Defined in this movie for the public, in regard to Stifler's mom.

**Where it pops up now.** Every modern teen comedy is in dialogue with this movie's formula.

---

## 89. The Sixth Sense (1999)

**Plot in plain language.** A child psychologist (Bruce Willis) treats a boy (Haley Joel Osment) who claims he can see dead people. M. Night Shyamalan's debut.

**Why it stuck.** The twist ending was a national event. Spoiler-protection was a sacred social contract for months.

**Quotes you'll actually hear.**
- "I see dead people." — Osment whispering to Willis. Used by anyone walking into a boring meeting.

**Where it pops up now.** Every plot-twist movie since is measured against this one.
